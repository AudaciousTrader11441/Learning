# General's Overview

## Overview

This is the executive view. It covers the state of the industry in February 2026, the hard numbers, what the Dark Factory means for competitive positioning, why tools alone will not close the gap, and what the new organisation looks like on the other side.

## Context

You are responsible for the entire operation. The questions below build on each other in sequence -- they tell the story from the current industry landscape through to the strategic decisions you need to make.

## The Industry Landscape

**Q: What is happening in the industry right now?**

Three realities coexist in February 2026:

1. **Frontier teams operate dark factories.** StrongDM's three-person team ships production software where no human writes or reviews code. Claude Code's own codebase is 90% written by Claude Code. Codex 5.3 was the first frontier model instrumental in creating itself.
2. **Most organisations are stuck.** A randomised controlled trial found experienced developers got 19% *slower* with AI tools -- while believing they were 20% faster.
3. **The gap is widening.** The distance between these realities is a specification gap, an organisational gap, and a talent gap. No software update closes it automatically.

**Q: What do the numbers say?**

| Data point | Source |
|------------|--------|
| TELUS deployed AI across 57,000 employees; 47 large-scale solutions generated over $90 million in benefits | Google Cloud case study |
| Zapier reports 89% AI adoption across 360 people, with 800+ internal agents | Zapier |

Revenue per employee at AI-native startups exceeds typical SaaS benchmarks by five to ten times. Tiny teams, enormous output.

## The Dark Factory

**Q: What is the Dark Factory and why should I care?**

A Dark Factory is a system that turns specifications into working software with no human writing or reviewing code. Specification in, working software out. StrongDM is the most documented example.

You should care because this is the new competitive baseline for greenfield development. Teams of three are producing output that traditionally required teams of thirty. The bottleneck has moved from "can we build it" to "should we build it" -- and the organisations that answer that question well, with clear specifications and deep domain understanding, will outproduce everyone else by an order of magnitude.

**Q: Can we just buy better tools to close the gap?**

No. The gap is not primarily a technology gap. It is:

- A **specification gap** -- most organisations cannot describe what their software should do with enough precision for autonomous execution.
- An **organisational gap** -- org charts built for humans coordinating with humans become friction when agents do the implementation.
- A **talent gap** -- the skills that matter have shifted from implementation speed to systems thinking, customer intuition, and specification clarity.
- A **willingness-to-change gap** -- end-to-end process transformation is hard, expensive, politically contentious, and slow.

## The Legacy Problem

**Q: What about our existing systems?**

This is the honest counterweight to the dark factory hype. The vast majority of enterprise software is brownfield -- existing systems built over years, running in production, carrying real revenue. The specification does not exist. The documentation is wrong. The tests cover 30% of the code and the rest runs on institutional knowledge.

The migration path takes **years, not months**:

1. Use AI at Level 2-3 to accelerate existing work (where the J-curve dip happens).
2. Use AI to *document* what your system actually does -- generating specifications from code, building scenario suites.
3. Redesign CI/CD to handle AI-generated code at volume.
4. Begin shifting new development to Level 4-5 patterns while maintaining legacy in parallel.

Anyone telling you this takes quarters is selling something.

## The Talent Reckoning

**Q: What is happening to the talent market?**

The junior developer pipeline is collapsing. Early-career developer employment dropped 7-10% within six quarters of AI adoption. UK graduate technology roles fell 46% in 2024 with a further 53% drop projected by 2026. Some US datasets suggest junior developer postings have declined by more than 60%.

The career ladder is being hollowed out from below: seniors at the top, AI at the bottom, and a thinning middle where the learning used to happen. Yet the need for *excellent* engineers has never been higher. The bar is rising toward systems thinking, customer intuition, and specification clarity -- the skills that have always separated great engineers from adequate ones. The difference now is that *adequate* is what the models do.

## The New Organisation

**Q: What does the new organisation look like?**

Flat. Small. Judgement-dense. Cursor hit $500M ARR with roughly forty people. Midjourney generated around $500M with under 163 people. The org chart flattens radically: the layers of coordination that manage hundreds of engineers become unnecessary when agents do the implementation.

The people who remain are the ones whose judgement cannot be automated: those who know *what* to build, *for whom*, and *why*. The constraint moves from "can we build it" to "should we build it" -- and that has always been the harder, more valuable question.

Drop the cost of software production by an order of magnitude and the unmet demand becomes addressable. The regional hospital, the mid-market manufacturer, the family logistics company -- they all need software they cannot afford at current labour costs. The total addressable market at 2027 production costs dwarfs the market at 2024 costs. The denominator is moving.
